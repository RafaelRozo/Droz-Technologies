## A Thousandth of a Millimetre Is Not a Bragging Right

It is the difference between winning a government aerospace contract and watching it go to a shop in Connecticut. CNC precision tolerances in Canada are not a marketing flourish — they are the entire competitive moat of the Canadian machining industry.

Ask any Ontario shop owner competing against imports from lower-cost jurisdictions: they are not winning on price per part. They are winning on whether the part is in spec the first time, every time, traceable, and certified. That is a tolerance story, and it has numbers behind it.

## Tolerance vs Precision — Stop Confusing the Two

The terms get mixed up in sales meetings constantly, and it costs people contracts.

**Tolerance** is the allowable deviation on a drawing. A dimension called out as 25.000 ±0.005 mm means the part is acceptable anywhere between 24.995 and 25.005 mm. It is a specification imposed by the designer.

**Precision** is the repeatability of your process. A machine that hits 25.0007 mm on twenty parts in a row is highly precise, regardless of whether that matches the nominal dimension.

**Accuracy** is how close you are to the nominal. You can be precise without being accurate (tight group, wrong spot). You can be accurate without being precise (right average, huge spread).

Canadian shops that understand the distinction run process capability studies — Cpk, Ppk — and can prove their machines hold tolerance with statistical confidence. Shops that can't, lose the audit.

## Where 0.001mm Actually Matters

Not every part needs micron-level work. Sell someone a ±0.001 mm part for a bracket that could accept ±0.1 mm and you are subsidizing their supply chain. The sectors where it genuinely matters in Canada:

### Aerospace
Landing gear components, turbine blades, fuel system parts. Canadian aerospace (Bombardier, Magellan, Héroux-Devtek, and their tier-2 Ontario suppliers) runs AS9100 quality systems that demand documented process capability. Miss tolerance, lose the approval.

### Medical devices
Implants, surgical instruments, catheters. Health Canada and FDA audits go after tolerance drift. Canadian med-device machining is one of the highest-margin sectors in Ontario for exactly this reason — it is defensible against imports.

### Optics
Lens mounts, telescope components, semiconductor lithography hardware. Alignment tolerances drop into the single-digit micron range.

### Semiconductor tooling
Canada is not a chip-fab country, but Canadian shops supply precision components into the global semiconductor supply chain — chucks, end effectors, chamber hardware. Tolerances here are often tighter than aerospace.

### Defence and government
Federal procurement under the Canadian Surface Combatant program and similar contracts specifies tolerances directly traceable to ISO and NIST standards. No traceability, no contract.

## How Canadian Shops Compete With Imports

The honest answer: not on cost per part. A comparable part machined offshore will almost always arrive cheaper at the dock. Canadian shops win on five things:

1. **First-pass yield** — low scrap rate saves money even at higher per-hour shop rates
2. **Lead time** — two weeks vs twelve weeks makes or breaks tight programs
3. **Traceability** — every part has a documented material cert, process record, and inspection report
4. **Engineering collaboration** — Canadian shops that can redline a drawing and propose a DFM change win repeat business
5. **Regulatory alignment** — AS9100, ISO 13485, ISO 9001, ITAR/CGP compliance in one place

Tolerance is the thread running through all five. A shop that can't hold it can't play.

## The Calibration Standards That Actually Matter

**[ISO 230-2 — Test code for machine tools, determination of accuracy and repeatability of positioning](https://www.iso.org/standard/55295.html)** is the reference standard for CNC positional accuracy. It defines how you measure, what axes, under what conditions, and how you report. If your calibration provider does not reference ISO 230-2 on their certificate, the certificate is not worth much in an audit.

Other standards you will encounter in Canada:
- **ISO 230-1** — geometric accuracy
- **ISO 230-3** — thermal effects (critical for shops without climate-controlled bays)
- **ISO 9283** — robotic positioning accuracy (for robotic cells)
- **B89.4.22** — CMM performance verification

Annual calibration against these standards is the baseline. Shops supplying aerospace or medical typically recalibrate quarterly or after any thermal event.

## Inspection Methods — and Their Realistic Limits

### Coordinate measuring machine (CMM)
The workhorse. A bridge-style CMM from Zeiss, Mitutoyo, or Hexagon will hold roughly 1.5-3 micron uncertainty in a good shop environment. Requires climate control (±1°C), foundation isolation, and a trained operator. Canadian shops often run their CMMs overnight in temperature-stabilized rooms for traceable first-article inspection.

### Laser interferometry
Used for machine tool calibration itself — not part inspection. A Renishaw XL-80 or equivalent can measure linear positional accuracy to sub-micron levels over a machine's travel. This is how you verify a machine meets ISO 230-2 after shipping, after a crash, or annually.

### Optical comparators and vision systems
Good for 2D features, flat parts, and quick first-article checks. Not suitable for anything requiring sub-5-micron confidence.

### Micrometers and gauge blocks
Still valid, still used daily. A good hand micrometer reads to 1 micron, but the operator variability (temperature, feel, technique) often dominates. Trust the CMM for audit evidence.

### In-process probing
Renishaw or Blum probes on the spindle. Great for closed-loop tolerance compensation mid-cycle. Not a replacement for final inspection.

## Case: A Burlington Shop Supplying Canadian Aerospace

A Burlington machine shop with 22 employees runs a mix of 5-axis Mazak and DMG Mori machines supplying aerospace components into the Canadian defence and commercial aviation supply chain. They hold AS9100 and run a full ISO 230-2 calibration program.

Their customers specify tolerances down to ±0.005 mm on critical dimensions and ±0.001 mm on certain bearing fits. Their Cpk on those features consistently reads above 1.67 — the aerospace benchmark for "capable with margin."

The shop's winning metric is not cost. It is first-pass yield: 99.4% across 14,000 parts per year. Every 0.1% improvement in yield is roughly $85,000 to the bottom line because scrap on high-value aerospace parts is brutally expensive. The tolerance discipline pays directly for itself.

They have never lost a government contract on price. They have lost exactly two on capacity.

## Tolerance Creep Is How Margins Die

Most shops don't lose tolerance overnight. They lose it gradually:

- A spindle bearing that is 80% of end-of-life but still "running fine"
- A ball screw with 4 microns of backlash accumulated over three years
- A coolant temperature that drifts 4°C between morning and afternoon
- An operator who has been "eyeballing" tool offsets instead of probing

Each one shaves a few microns off your capability. Together, they turn a ±0.005 mm shop into a ±0.015 mm shop, and suddenly the aerospace auditor is asking questions you cannot answer. By the time scrap rates climb, the damage is a full quarter of lost margin.

The fix is boring and it works: scheduled calibration, documented process capability studies, spindle health monitoring, and thermal stabilization. Tolerance is maintained, not achieved.

## The Contrarian Take

Canadian shops don't need to chase tighter tolerances. They need to prove the tolerances they already hit. The gap between "our machines can do it" and "here is the audited Cpk data showing we do it every time" is where most shops lose aerospace and medical contracts. Invest in metrology and documentation before you invest in more machines.
