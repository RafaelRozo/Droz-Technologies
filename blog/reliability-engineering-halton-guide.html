Step 1: Conduct an Asset Criticality Analysis

You can't improve everything at once. Criticality analysis ranks your equipment by the consequences of failure—safety, environmental, production, and cost—so you focus resources where they matter most.

How to Run a Criticality Assessment

  1. Build your asset register. List every piece of equipment with a unique identifier. If you're running a CMMS, export it. If you're on spreadsheets, now is the time to consolidate.
  2. Define criticality criteria. Score each asset on: safety impact (1–5), environmental risk (1–5), production impact (1–5), repair cost (1–5), and failure frequency (1–5).
  3. Calculate a criticality index. Multiply scores or use a weighted sum. Assets scoring in the top 20% are your "critical" tier.
  4. Validate with operations. Numbers don't capture everything. Walk the floor with operators and supervisors to sanity-check rankings.
A Burlington food manufacturer we worked with discovered their most "critical" asset wasn't the main production line—it was a refrigeration compressor whose failure would spoil $300K in inventory within hours.

Step 2: Apply RCM Methodology

Reliability-Centered Maintenance (RCM) is the gold standard framework for deciding what maintenance to do, and when. Developed originally for aviation, RCM in Ontario manufacturing has gained traction as plants seek to move beyond calendar-based PM schedules that either over-maintain cheap assets or under-maintain critical ones.

RCM answers seven questions for each critical asset:

  1. What are the functions of this asset in its current operating context?
  2. In what ways can it fail to fulfil those functions (functional failures)?
  3. What causes each functional failure (failure modes)?
  4. What happens when each failure occurs (failure effects)?
  5. In what way does each failure matter (failure consequences)?
  6. What can be done to predict or prevent each failure?
  7. What should be done if a suitable proactive task cannot be found?

The output is a maintenance strategy tailored to each failure mode: predictive monitoring, scheduled restoration, scheduled replacement, or conscious run-to-failure for non-critical items.

Step 3: Perform FMEA on Critical Assets

Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is the analytical engine inside RCM. For each critical asset, your team documents every plausible failure mode, its cause, its effect on operations, and assigns a Risk Priority Number (RPN).

Focus on failure modes with the highest RPNs first. These are your quick wins—the failures that are most likely, most severe, and hardest to detect. Assign a proactive maintenance task or redesign to each one.

Tip: run FMEA workshops with cross-functional teams. Operators, maintenance techs, and engineers together will identify failure modes that no single group would catch alone.

Step 4: Establish Your KPIs

You can't manage what you don't measure. Four KPIs form the foundation of any plant reliability program:

MTBF

Mean Time Between Failures. Total operating time ÷ number of failures. Measures equipment reliability. Higher is better.

MTTR

Mean Time To Repair. Total repair time ÷ number of repairs. Measures maintenance efficiency. Lower is better.

OEE

Overall Equipment Effectiveness. Availability × Performance × Quality. World-class is 85%+. Most plants start at 55–65%.

PM Compliance

Preventive Maintenance Compliance. PMs completed on time ÷ PMs scheduled. Target 90%+ before adding predictive strategies.

Setting Baselines

Before setting targets, measure where you are today. Run a 90-day data collection period. If your CMMS data is unreliable, start with manual tracking on critical assets. Imperfect data today is infinitely more valuable than perfect data you'll collect "someday."

For plant reliability in the Toronto and Greater Toronto Area, we typically see these starting benchmarks in mid-size facilities:

  • OEE: 55–65% (world-class target: 85%)
  • MTBF on critical assets: 200–500 hours
  • MTTR: 2–6 hours
  • PM Compliance: 60–75%

Step 5: Build the Team and Culture

Technology and methodology matter, but reliability programs live or die on people. You need three things:

A Reliability Champion

Designate one person—a reliability engineer, a senior maintenance planner, or even a motivated supervisor—as the program owner. This person drives FMEA workshops, tracks KPIs, and keeps momentum between competing priorities.

Operator Involvement

Operators are your front-line sensors. Train them on basic autonomous maintenance: cleaning, inspection, lubrication, and early-warning detection. A ten-minute daily inspection by an operator who knows their machine catches problems that vibration sensors miss.

Management Commitment

Reliability engineering requires upfront investment in time, training, and sometimes technology. Leadership must protect the program from being the first budget line cut when quarterly targets feel tight. Frame it in language leadership understands: reduced overtime, fewer emergency part orders, improved on-time delivery, and lower insurance risk.

Step 6: Layer in Predictive Technologies

Once your foundation is solid—criticality ranked, FMEAs done on top assets, KPIs tracking, PM compliance above 85%—layer in condition-based monitoring:

  • Vibration analysis for rotating equipment (motors, pumps, fans)
  • Infrared thermography for electrical panels and mechanical assemblies
  • Oil analysis for hydraulic systems and gearboxes
  • Ultrasonic testing for compressed air leaks (the average plant wastes 20–30% of compressed air to leaks)

You don't need to buy every sensor on day one. Start with vibration monitoring on your top 10 critical assets. Many Ontario-based service providers offer route-based monitoring on a contract basis, so you can access the technology without the capital expenditure.

Step 7: Implement a CMMS That Works for You

A Computerized Maintenance Management System is the nervous system of your reliability program. It stores asset data, schedules PMs, tracks work orders, and generates the KPI reports you need.

Common options for mid-size Ontario manufacturers include Fiix (Canadian-built, now part of Rockwell Automation), UpKeep, and Limble. The best CMMS is the one your team will actually use. Prioritize mobile access, ease of work-order creation, and reporting over feature count.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Boiling the ocean. Don't FMEA every asset. Start with the critical 20% that drive 80% of your downtime.
  • Skipping the baseline. Without knowing where you started, you can't prove improvement—and you'll lose executive support.
  • Over-investing in technology. Sensors and software don't fix broken processes. Nail the fundamentals first.
  • Ignoring the culture shift. A reliability program is a change management initiative as much as a technical one.
  • Treating it as a project with an end date. Reliability engineering is a continuous discipline, not a one-time effort.

The Bottom Line

Building a reliability engineering program isn't glamorous. It's methodical, iterative work—but it's work that compounds. Plants that commit to RCM methodology, rigorous FMEA, and disciplined KPI tracking routinely achieve 25–40% reductions in unplanned downtime within 18 months.

For Halton Region manufacturers competing in a tight Ontario market, that improvement can be the difference between winning contracts and losing them.
