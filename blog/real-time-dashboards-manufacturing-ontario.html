Real-Time Dashboards for Manufacturing: Connecting Shop Floor to C-Suite

By Ricardo Rozo, CTO at Droz Technologies · February 24, 2026 · 9 min read

Walk into most Ontario manufacturing plants and you'll find the same disconnect: operators know their machines intimately, but the data lives on clipboards, whiteboards, and spreadsheets that are already stale by the time anyone in management sees them. Meanwhile, the C-suite makes capital decisions based on monthly reports that lag reality by weeks.

Real-time dashboards eliminate that gap. They turn raw machine signals into live, visual intelligence that every level of the organization can act on—from the technician watching vibration trends to the CFO tracking overall equipment effectiveness (OEE) across three plants.

In this post, we'll walk through the architecture that makes this work: data pipelines, industrial protocols like MQTT and OPC-UA, visualization with Grafana, intelligent alerting, and the ROI that justifies the investment.

Why Manufacturing Needs Real-Time Visibility

The Halton and Greater Toronto manufacturing corridor is one of the most productive in Canada. But productivity gains are plateauing for companies still relying on batch reporting. Here's what real-time analytics changes:

  • Downtime response drops from hours to minutes. When a CNC spindle starts drifting out of tolerance, an alert fires before the first scrap part comes off the line.
  • Energy waste becomes visible. A compressor running at 80% load during a weekend shutdown is invisible in a monthly utility bill. On a live dashboard, it's obvious.
  • Shift handoffs get data-driven. Instead of verbal summaries, the incoming shift sees exactly what happened—throughput curves, alarm history, quality trends.
  • Executive decisions accelerate. Capital expenditure conversations move from "we think Line 3 is the bottleneck" to "Line 3 OEE has been 12 points below target for 14 consecutive days—here's the breakdown."

The Data Pipeline: From Sensor to Screen

A manufacturing dashboard is only as good as the data feeding it. The pipeline has four stages:

1. Data Acquisition

Modern PLCs (Siemens S7, Allen-Bradley, Mitsubishi) expose data through OPC-UA, the industry-standard protocol for machine-to-machine communication. OPC-UA provides structured, secure, platform-independent access to variables like cycle counts, temperatures, pressures, and fault codes.

For simpler sensors—temperature probes, vibration monitors, power meters—MQTT is the go-to. It's a lightweight publish-subscribe protocol designed for constrained devices. A $15 ESP32 with a current transformer can publish power readings every second over MQTT.

2. Message Broker

An MQTT broker (Mosquitto, EMQX, or HiveMQ) acts as the central nervous system. Machines publish to topics like plant/line3/press02/temperature, and any number of subscribers—databases, dashboards, alerting engines—can consume those messages independently.

For OPC-UA sources, a connector like Kepware or the open-source node-opcua bridges PLC data into MQTT topics or writes directly to the time-series database.

3. Time-Series Storage

Manufacturing data is inherently temporal. You need a database optimized for high-frequency writes and time-range queries. The leading choices:

  • InfluxDB — Purpose-built for time-series. Great query language (Flux), built-in retention policies, and native Grafana integration.
  • TimescaleDB — PostgreSQL extension. Choose this if your team already knows SQL and you want relational joins with time-series performance.
  • QuestDB — Blazing fast ingest. Ideal for plants generating millions of data points per minute.

4. Visualization and Dashboards

This is where Grafana shines. It connects natively to all three databases above and provides:

  • Customizable panels: gauges, heatmaps, time-series graphs, tables, alerts
  • Role-based access: operators see their line, plant managers see aggregates, executives see cross-plant KPIs
  • Templating: one dashboard template serves all production lines with variable selectors
  • Mobile-responsive layouts for tablet-mounted shop floor displays

Architecture in Practice

Here's a typical deployment we've built for Ontario manufacturers:

Sensors & PLCs → OPC-UA / MQTT → Mosquitto Broker → Telegraf (collector) → InfluxDB → Grafana → Alerts (Slack, PagerDuty, SMS)

The entire stack can run on a single on-premise server for plants with air-gapped networks, or on cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure) for multi-site visibility. Hybrid deployments—edge processing on-site with cloud aggregation—are increasingly common.

A key design principle: decouple collection from visualization. The broker and database should keep ingesting data even if Grafana goes down for maintenance. And the alerting engine should operate independently of the dashboard—nobody should miss a critical alarm because a browser tab was closed.

Intelligent Alerting: Beyond Simple Thresholds

The simplest alert is a threshold: temperature above 85°C triggers a warning. But manufacturing environments demand more nuance:

  • Rate-of-change alerts: A bearing temperature rising 2°C per minute is more urgent than one sitting steady at 82°C.
  • Anomaly detection: Machine learning models trained on historical data can flag when a vibration signature deviates from its normal pattern—even if it hasn't crossed a fixed threshold.
  • Composite conditions: Alert only when motor current is high AND coolant flow is low AND the machine has been running for more than 4 hours. This reduces false positives dramatically.
  • Escalation chains: Notify the line operator first. If unacknowledged in 10 minutes, escalate to the maintenance supervisor. After 30 minutes, page the plant manager.

Grafana's alerting engine handles threshold and composite rules natively. For anomaly detection, we typically pair it with a lightweight Python service running scikit-learn or Facebook Prophet, writing anomaly scores back into InfluxDB for Grafana to visualize and alert on.

What Different Stakeholders See

Shop Floor Operators

Large-format displays mounted near production lines showing real-time cycle times, current production count vs. target, and active alarms. Color-coded: green (on target), yellow (falling behind), red (stopped). No login required—these are read-only kiosk dashboards.

Maintenance Teams

Vibration trends, temperature histories, motor current draws, and predictive maintenance scores. Drill-down capability to see individual sensor readings. Integration with the CMMS (computerized maintenance management system) to auto-generate work orders when thresholds are breached.

Plant Managers

OEE breakdown (availability × performance × quality) by line, shift comparison charts, downtime Pareto analysis, and energy consumption trends. Weekly and monthly roll-ups with export-to-PDF for stakeholder meetings.

C-Suite Executives

Cross-plant KPI summary: total output, cost per unit, OEE benchmarks, capital utilization rates. Financial overlay showing production costs against revenue targets. Minimal detail, maximum clarity—designed to answer "are we on track?" in under 10 seconds.

ROI: Making the Business Case

Manufacturing dashboards aren't a cost center—they're a profit lever. Here's how to quantify it:

Downtime reduction. The average cost of unplanned downtime in discrete manufacturing is $5,000–$20,000 per hour (depending on the operation). A dashboard with predictive alerting that prevents even one major incident per quarter pays for itself.

OEE improvement. A 5-point OEE increase on a line producing $10M annually translates to roughly $500K in additional output—without adding equipment or headcount.

Energy savings. Real-time power monitoring typically reveals 10–20% waste from equipment running outside production hours, compressed air leaks, and inefficient load scheduling. For a plant spending $400K annually on electricity, that's $40K–$80K recovered.

Quality improvements. Catching process drift early reduces scrap rates. A 2% reduction in scrap on a high-volume line can save $100K+ per year in material costs alone.

Typical investment: $30K–$80K for a pilot covering one production line (hardware, software, integration, training). Full plant rollouts range from $100K–$300K depending on scale. Payback period: 4–8 months.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Dashboard overload. Fifty panels on one screen helps nobody. Design for the decision each stakeholder needs to make, not the data you can collect.
  • Ignoring network infrastructure. Industrial Ethernet and Wi-Fi in a plant full of metal and EMI is not the same as an office network. Budget for proper industrial networking.
  • Skipping the data model. Define naming conventions and tag structures before connecting the first sensor. Retrofitting a messy tag namespace across 200 machines is painful.
  • No change management. The best dashboard is useless if operators don't trust it. Involve floor staff early, incorporate their input on what metrics matter, and train thoroughly.

Getting Started

You don't need to instrument an entire plant on day one. The most successful deployments start small:

  1. Pick one pain point. The line with the most downtime, the process with the highest scrap rate, or the area with the biggest energy bill.
  2. Audit existing data sources. Many modern PLCs already have OPC-UA enabled—you may be sitting on data you're not using.
  3. Deploy a pilot in 4–6 weeks. Broker, database, Grafana, and a handful of dashboards. Prove value fast.
  4. Expand based on results. Once the first line shows measurable improvement, the business case for the next five lines writes itself.

Ontario's manufacturing sector is under pressure from global competition, rising energy costs, and labour shortages. Real-time dashboards won't solve all of those problems—but they give you the visibility to solve them faster, with data instead of gut feel.
