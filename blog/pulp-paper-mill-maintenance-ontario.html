Predictive Maintenance for Pulp & Paper Mills: Reduce Unplanned Downtime by 70%

By Rafael Rozo, CEO of Droz Technologies  |  February 24, 2026  |  8 min read

A single hour of unplanned downtime on a paper machine can cost between $20,000 and $80,000. Multiply that across the dozens of unplanned stops a typical Ontario mill experiences each year, and the financial impact becomes staggering. Yet many pulp and paper operations across the province still rely on time-based maintenance schedules that were designed decades ago for simpler equipment.

After more than 20 years helping manufacturers across Ontario implement reliability programs, I have seen firsthand what separates high-performing mills from those constantly fighting fires. The difference is rarely the equipment itself—it is how they monitor and maintain it.

Predictive maintenance, when properly implemented, gives your maintenance team the ability to detect developing failures weeks or even months before they cause a shutdown. The result for most mills is a 60–70% reduction in unplanned downtime and a meaningful improvement in overall equipment effectiveness.

Why Pulp & Paper Mills Are Uniquely Challenging

Paper machines are among the most complex continuous-process systems in manufacturing. A typical machine runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, with hundreds of rotating components operating in extreme conditions—high temperatures, moisture, heavy loads, and corrosive chemicals.

The consequences of failure cascade quickly. A single bearing seizure on a dryer cylinder does not just stop that cylinder—it stops the entire machine. Every minute of downtime affects production upstream in the stock preparation system and downstream in finishing and converting.

This interconnectedness makes pulp paper maintenance in Ontario especially critical. Mills that invest in condition monitoring across all major sections of the machine see dramatically better reliability than those monitoring only the most obvious failure points.

Dryer Section Monitoring: The Highest-Value Opportunity

The dryer section is the single largest concentration of rotating equipment on any paper machine. A typical machine has 40 to 80 dryer cylinders, each spinning continuously with steam pressures up to 150 psi and surface temperatures exceeding 350°F.

Bearing failures in the dryer section are the most common cause of unplanned stops, and they are also among the most preventable. Here is what an effective dryer monitoring program looks like:

Vibration Monitoring

Accelerometers mounted on dryer bearing housings detect changes in vibration signature long before a bearing reaches a critical failure state. We typically see a detectable change in vibration amplitude 4–8 weeks before failure, giving maintenance teams ample time to plan a replacement during a scheduled shutdown.

Wireless vibration sensors have transformed dryer section monitoring. Given the sheer number of bearings—often 100 or more across the dryer section—wired monitoring was historically cost-prohibitive. Modern wireless sensors with 3–5 year battery life make comprehensive coverage practical for the first time.

Infrared Thermography

Thermal imaging of dryer shells reveals condensate evacuation problems, siphon failures, and steam distribution issues that degrade drying efficiency before they cause mechanical damage. A dryer cylinder running 30°F cooler than its neighbours is a clear sign of a condensate removal problem that, left unaddressed, causes thermal cycling and eventual shell cracking.

Steam Joint and Siphon Integrity

Ultrasonic inspection of rotary steam joints identifies leaks and wear before they become safety hazards. Failed steam joints not only reduce drying capacity but can cause significant water damage to felts and machine clothing.

Real-world impact: One Ontario mill we worked with reduced dryer section-related stops from 14 per year to 3 within the first 12 months of implementing comprehensive vibration and thermal monitoring—an 80% reduction that translated to over $1.2 million in recovered production.

Press Roll Alignment and Nip Uniformity

The press section removes water mechanically before the sheet enters the dryers. Proper press roll alignment is critical for both paper quality and equipment life. Misalignment causes problems that compound rapidly:

  • Uneven nip pressure creates cross-machine moisture profile defects that cannot be corrected downstream
  • Accelerated felt wear increases clothing costs and causes more frequent felt changes
  • Bearing overloading on the high-pressure side leads to premature failure
  • Sheet breaks from uneven pressing reduce machine efficiency

Laser alignment surveys during scheduled shutdowns establish baseline conditions, while continuous vibration monitoring on press roll bearings detects developing misalignment between shutdowns. Nip impression testing—using pressure-sensitive film or electronic nip sensors—quantifies nip pressure uniformity across the full roll face.

For manufacturing maintenance in Halton and the broader GTA, where several major converting and packaging mills operate, press section reliability directly impacts product quality consistency. Customers in food packaging and pharmaceutical markets have zero tolerance for moisture-related sheet defects.

Pump Reliability: The Overlooked Foundation

A typical paper mill operates 200 to 400 pumps across stock preparation, the approach system, chemical preparation, white water handling, and utilities. Pumps are so ubiquitous that they often receive less monitoring attention than they deserve—until a critical pump failure brings the entire operation down.

The most common pump failure modes in pulp and paper applications include:

  • Bearing degradation from contamination, misalignment, or inadequate lubrication
  • Seal failures from cavitation, dry running, or abrasive stock
  • Impeller erosion from high-consistency stock or foreign material
  • Cavitation damage from improper suction conditions or system changes

A tiered monitoring approach works best: continuous online monitoring for the 15–20 most critical pumps (fan pump, primary headbox pump, seal water pumps), route-based vibration collection for the next tier, and ultrasonic screening for the remainder.

Oil analysis adds another dimension to pump reliability. Quarterly oil samples from major pump gearboxes and bearing housings detect contamination, wear metal generation, and lubricant degradation trends that vibration monitoring alone can miss.

Bearing Failures: Patterns and Prevention

Across all equipment types, bearing failures remain the single largest cause of unplanned downtime in paper mills. Our data from Ontario facilities shows that bearing-related events account for approximately 40% of all unplanned mechanical stops.

The good news is that bearings rarely fail without warning. A properly instrumented bearing gives 4–12 weeks of detectable warning before catastrophic failure. The key is knowing what to look for:

  1. Stage 1 (subsurface fatigue): Detectable only with high-frequency enveloping or acoustic emission—ultrasonic range indicators begin trending upward
  2. Stage 2 (microscopic spalling): Bearing defect frequencies appear in the vibration spectrum; this is the ideal time to plan replacement
  3. Stage 3 (visible damage): Broadband vibration increases, temperature begins to rise; replacement should be scheduled urgently
  4. Stage 4 (imminent failure): High vibration, elevated temperature, audible noise; emergency replacement required

Catching failures at Stage 2 rather than Stage 4 is the difference between a planned 2-hour bearing swap and a 24-hour emergency repair that includes collateral damage to shafts, housings, and seals.

Building a Paper Mill Reliability Program

Effective paper mill reliability does not happen by purchasing sensors. It requires a structured program that integrates technology, processes, and people. Here is the framework we use with Ontario mills:

  1. Asset criticality ranking: Identify the 20% of equipment that causes 80% of your downtime and lost production. Start monitoring there.
  2. Baseline data collection: Establish normal operating signatures for every monitored asset. Without a baseline, you cannot identify degradation.
  3. Alarm and action thresholds: Set alert levels based on ISO 10816 standards, bearing manufacturer specifications, and your own historical data.
  4. Integration with planning: Connect your monitoring system to your CMMS so that condition-based work orders flow directly into the maintenance schedule.
  5. Continuous improvement: Review every unplanned stop. Was it on a monitored asset? If so, why was it missed? If not, should it be monitored?

Frequently Asked Questions

How much downtime can predictive maintenance eliminate in a pulp and paper mill?

A well-implemented program typically reduces unplanned downtime by 60–70%. By monitoring dryer bearings, press rolls, and pumps continuously, mills detect failures weeks in advance and schedule repairs during planned shutdowns.

What are the most failure-prone components in a paper mill?

Dryer section bearings, press roll journals, vacuum pump seals, stock preparation refiners, and felt conditioning equipment. Bearing failures alone account for roughly 40% of unplanned downtime events in typical mills.

What monitoring technologies are used for paper mill dryer sections?

Vibration analysis with accelerometers on bearing housings, infrared thermography for shell temperature profiling, ultrasonic testing for steam joint integrity, and oil analysis. Wireless sensors are increasingly used due to the high number of rotating cylinders.

How does press roll alignment affect paper quality and machine reliability?

Misaligned press rolls create uneven nip pressure, causing sheet moisture profile defects, accelerated felt wear, and premature bearing failure. Laser alignment and nip impression testing identify misalignment before it causes quality defects or catastrophic damage.

What is the ROI of predictive maintenance for Ontario paper mills?

Most mills see ROI within 6–12 months. With unplanned downtime costing $20,000–$80,000 per hour, preventing even a single catastrophic failure can pay for an entire annual monitoring program.
