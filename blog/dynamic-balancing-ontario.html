What Is Dynamic Balancing and Why It Matters

Every rotating component has some degree of mass asymmetry. Manufacturing tolerances, material inconsistencies, erosion, deposit buildup, and thermal distortion all contribute to unbalance. When a rotor spins, this uneven mass distribution generates centrifugal force proportional to the square of rotational speed. Double the speed, and you quadruple the unbalance force.

Dynamic balancing is the process of detecting the magnitude and angular location of this excess mass, then adding or removing material to bring the rotor within acceptable residual unbalance limits. The result: dramatically lower vibration, longer bearing life, reduced energy consumption, and quieter operation.

Understanding ISO 1940: The International Balancing Standard

ISO 1940-1 (Mechanical vibration — Balance quality requirements for rotors in a constant rigid state) is the global benchmark for specifying acceptable residual unbalance. Every professional dynamic balancing service in Ontario should reference this standard.

Balance Quality Grades Explained

ISO 1940 defines balance quality grades using the notation "G" followed by a number representing the permissible velocity of vibration at the bearing in mm/s. Lower G numbers mean tighter tolerances:

Gradee·ω (mm/s)Typical Applications
G0.40.4Precision grinding spindles, gyroscopes
G1.01.0Turbo generators, turbo compressors
G2.52.5Gas and steam turbines, electric motors, pumps, fans
G6.36.3Process plant machinery, centrifuges, general industrial fans
G1616Agricultural equipment, crusher drives
G4040Car wheels, automotive crankshafts (assembled)

Calculating Permissible Residual Unbalance

The permissible residual unbalance (Uper) is calculated as:

Uper = (G × M) / ω

Where G is the balance quality grade (mm/s), M is the rotor mass (kg), and ω is the angular velocity (rad/s) at maximum operating speed. For a two-plane correction, the permissible unbalance is distributed between the two correction planes based on the rotor geometry.

For example, a 500 kg fan rotor operating at 1,800 RPM requiring G2.5:

  • ω = (1,800 × 2π) / 60 = 188.5 rad/s
  • Uper = (2.5 × 500) / 188.5 = 6.63 g·mm

Single-Plane vs Two-Plane Dynamic Balancing

Choosing the correct balancing method depends on the rotor's geometry, specifically its length-to-diameter (L/D) ratio.

Single-Plane (Static) Balancing

Single-plane balancing corrects unbalance by adding or removing mass in one correction plane. It's appropriate when:

  • The rotor L/D ratio is less than 0.5 (disc-shaped rotors)
  • The dominant unbalance is static (the heavy spot is centered along the rotor length)
  • Applications include: impellers, flywheels, grinding wheels, single-stage compressor wheels, pulleys, and coupling hubs

The process requires one vibration measurement per trial run. A trial weight is applied, vibration is re-measured, and vector calculations determine the correct weight magnitude and placement angle.

Two-Plane (Dynamic) Balancing

Two-plane balancing simultaneously corrects static and couple unbalance using two correction planes. It's required when:

  • The rotor L/D ratio exceeds 0.5 (cylindrical or elongated rotors)
  • Couple unbalance is present (the principal axis of inertia is tilted relative to the shaft axis)
  • Applications include: motor rotors, multi-stage pump rotors, induced draft fans, turbine rotors, rolls, and generator rotors

Two-plane balancing requires measurements at both bearing locations and two trial runs (one weight in each plane). The influence coefficient method calculates corrections for both planes simultaneously, accounting for cross-effect between planes.

Expert Tip: Many Ontario plants attempt single-plane balancing on rotors that require two-plane correction. This typically results in a "whack-a-mole" scenario where correcting vibration at one bearing worsens it at the other. If you're not achieving satisfactory results, contact Droz Technologies—the issue may be couple unbalance requiring a two-plane approach.

Field Balancing vs Shop Balancing: Choosing the Right Approach

Field Balancing: Minimal Downtime, Maximum Convenience

Field balancing is performed on-site with the rotor installed in its own bearings and driven at normal operating speed. Droz Technologies field balancing services across Ontario use portable single-channel and dual-channel analyzers with real-time balancing software.

Advantages of field balancing:

  • No disassembly required—saves 4–16 hours of downtime versus shop balancing
  • Rotor is balanced in its actual operating condition (temperature, alignment, bearing condition all factored in)
  • Can correct assembly-induced unbalance (coupling, key effects)
  • Lower total cost for most in-situ equipment
  • Typical completion time: 2–4 hours for single-plane, 3–6 hours for two-plane

Best applications: fans, blowers, centrifugal pumps, motors, generators, chipper discs, and any equipment where removal is difficult or risky.

Shop Balancing: Maximum Precision

Shop balancing uses a dedicated hard-bearing or soft-bearing balancing machine. The rotor is removed, transported to the shop, and balanced under controlled conditions.

Advantages of shop balancing:

  • Higher achievable precision (G1.0 and better)
  • Allows overspeed testing for critical rotors
  • Multi-plane balancing (3+ planes) is practical
  • Can detect and correct bent shafts, looseness, and other mechanical issues during the process
  • Required for new or rebuilt rotors before installation

Best applications: turbo generator rotors, turbine components, rebuilt motors, new impellers, and any rotor requiring G1.0 or better tolerance.

The Dynamic Balancing Process: Step by Step

Whether performed in the field or the shop, professional dynamic balancing follows a systematic process:

Step 1: Initial Vibration Assessment

Before balancing, a complete vibration survey confirms that unbalance is the primary problem. The vibration spectrum must show a dominant 1× RPM (running speed) peak. If other frequencies dominate—2×, bearing tones, blade pass—those issues must be addressed first.

Step 2: Reference Measurement

Baseline vibration amplitude and phase readings are taken at each bearing in the horizontal, vertical, and axial directions. For field balancing, a phase reference (tachometer or optical sensor) is essential.

Step 3: Trial Weight Application

A known trial weight is attached at a known angular position on the rotor. The mass of the trial weight is estimated based on rotor weight—typically 5–10% of the expected correction weight, positioned at the maximum correction radius.

Step 4: Trial Run Measurement

The machine is brought to operating speed and vibration amplitude/phase readings are taken with the trial weight installed. The change in vibration (influence coefficient) tells the analyzer how the rotor responds to mass at that location.

Step 5: Correction Calculation

Using single-plane or two-plane vector calculations, the software determines the exact correction weight and angle. Modern portable analyzers perform this calculation instantly.

Step 6: Correction and Verification

The trial weight is removed, the calculated correction weight is permanently attached (welding, bolting, or set screws), and a final verification run confirms the balance quality meets the ISO 1940 target grade.

Step 7: Documentation

When to Schedule Dynamic Balancing

Don't wait for catastrophic failure. Schedule dynamic balancing when:

  • Vibration analysis detects elevated 1× RPM: Your predictive maintenance program's vibration trending should flag increasing 1× amplitudes
  • After component replacement: New impellers, coupling changes, key modifications—any mass change requires re-balancing
  • During planned outages: Incorporate balancing into turnaround scopes for critical equipment
  • Post-repair: After rotor repairs, weld buildup, or machining that alters mass distribution
  • New installations: Verify balance quality meets specification before commissioning
  • After erosion or deposit buildup: Fans in dusty environments and pump impellers handling slurries accumulate mass changes over time

Frequently Asked Questions

What is dynamic balancing and why is it critical for Ontario manufacturers?

Dynamic balancing corrects mass distribution in rotating components so the center of mass aligns with the rotation axis. For Ontario manufacturers, unbalance causes roughly 40% of all vibration problems—leading to premature bearing failure, seal damage, energy waste, and unplanned downtime costing thousands per hour.

What ISO standard governs rotor balancing quality?

ISO 1940-1 defines balance quality grades from G0.4 (precision spindles) to G4000. Most Ontario industrial equipment requires G2.5 to G6.3. The standard calculates permissible residual unbalance based on rotor mass and maximum operating speed.

What is the difference between single-plane and two-plane balancing?

Single-plane balancing corrects unbalance in one plane for disc-shaped rotors (L/D < 0.5)—impellers, flywheels, and pulleys. Two-plane balancing corrects both static and couple unbalance for elongated rotors (L/D > 0.5)—motors, multi-stage pumps, fans, and turbines.

Should I choose field balancing or shop balancing?

Field balancing is performed on-site and eliminates disassembly—ideal for fans, blowers, and pumps that are difficult to remove. Shop balancing achieves higher precision (G1.0+) and is preferred for rebuilt components or when overspeed testing is required.

How much does unbalanced equipment cost Ontario plants?

Unbalanced equipment reduces bearing life by 50–80%, increases energy costs by 5–10%, and causes unplanned shutdowns. For a single critical machine, this can cost $15,000–$75,000 per year. Balancing typically delivers ROI exceeding 10:1.

What vibration levels indicate equipment needs balancing?

Per ISO 10816-3, vibration above 4.5 mm/s RMS on Class III machines signals unsatisfactory condition. When a dominant 1× RPM peak confirms unbalance, dynamic balancing should be performed. Droz Technologies recommends action when 1× exceeds 3.5 mm/s RMS.
