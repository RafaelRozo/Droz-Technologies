Oil & Gas Equipment Reliability: Condition Monitoring for Ontario Refineries & Pipelines

By Rafael Rozo, CEO of Droz Technologies  |  February 24, 2026  |  9 min read

Ontario's oil and gas sector operates some of the most safety-critical equipment in Canadian industry. From the refining complexes in Sarnia—often called Chemical Valley—to the pipeline networks that cross the province, equipment reliability is not just an economic concern. It is a safety imperative.

An unplanned compressor shutdown at a refinery can cascade into a full unit trip, costing hundreds of thousands of dollars per day in lost throughput. A pump seal failure on a hydrocarbon service can create a loss-of-containment event with serious safety and environmental consequences. These are not hypothetical scenarios—they are the realities that maintenance and reliability engineers manage every day.

Over more than two decades working with oil and gas facilities, I have seen how the right condition monitoring program transforms operations from reactive firefighting to proactive reliability management. This article covers the critical equipment, standards, and monitoring strategies that Ontario refineries and pipeline operators need to get right.

API Standards: The Foundation of Oil & Gas Reliability

Unlike many other industries where monitoring practices are largely discretionary, the oil and gas sector operates under a well-defined framework of API (American Petroleum Institute) standards that establish minimum requirements for equipment monitoring and protection.

Understanding these standards is essential for any oil gas maintenance program in Ontario:

API 670: Machinery Protection Systems

API 670 is the cornerstone standard for continuous monitoring of critical rotating equipment. It specifies requirements for radial vibration, axial position, bearing temperature, and speed monitoring on turbomachinery. Key provisions include:

  • Dual-voting logic for automatic trip systems—two sensors must agree before a machine shuts down, preventing spurious trips from a single sensor fault
  • Proximity probe installations with specific mounting requirements for shaft-relative vibration measurement on fluid-film bearing machines
  • Temperature monitoring of bearing pads and oil drain temperatures with alarm and trip setpoints
  • Axial position monitoring to detect thrust bearing degradation before catastrophic thrust failure

Compliance with API 670 is not optional for major rotating equipment in refinery service. Insurance underwriters, regulatory bodies, and internal safety reviews all expect adherence to these requirements.

Equipment-Specific Standards

Beyond API 670, several equipment-specific standards define design, testing, and monitoring requirements:

  • API 617 — Axial and centrifugal compressors: covers performance testing, rotor dynamics, and monitoring requirements for these high-value assets
  • API 618 — Reciprocating compressors: addresses pulsation control, vibration limits, and condition monitoring for reciprocating machines
  • API 610 — Centrifugal pumps: defines design requirements including minimum bearing life, allowable vibration levels, and NPSH requirements
  • API 612 — Steam turbines: covers special-purpose steam turbines used as compressor and pump drivers

Compressor Monitoring: Protecting Your Most Valuable Assets

Compressors are typically the most expensive and operationally critical rotating equipment in a refinery. A single centrifugal compressor train—driver, gearbox, and compressor—can represent a $5–15 million capital investment, and an unplanned failure can take 6–12 months to repair if a new rotor is required.

Centrifugal Compressors

Centrifugal compressors in refinery service (hydrogen recycle, reformer charge, wet gas, FCC main air blower) require comprehensive monitoring that goes beyond basic vibration:

  • Shaft vibration (radial): Proximity probes per API 670, monitoring both amplitude and phase for balance condition, alignment, and rotor dynamic behaviour
  • Axial position: Thrust probe monitoring is critical—thrust bearing failures can destroy an entire rotor assembly in seconds
  • Bearing temperature: Embedded RTDs in tilting-pad journal and thrust bearings provide early detection of lubrication film breakdown
  • Process parameters: Suction and discharge pressure, temperature, and flow data are essential for distinguishing mechanical problems from process-induced operating issues like surge
  • Oil analysis: Monthly oil samples detect wear metals, varnish formation, and contamination before they affect bearing performance

Critical insight: Surge is one of the most destructive events a centrifugal compressor can experience. Anti-surge control systems prevent it, but monitoring surge margin trending helps identify when process changes are pushing the compressor closer to its stability limit—before the anti-surge valve has to intervene.

Reciprocating Compressors

Reciprocating compressors present a different monitoring challenge. Their inherently high vibration levels and complex valve trains require specialized techniques:

  • Valve temperature monitoring: Thermocouples on each valve cap detect leaking valves before they cause capacity loss or cylinder damage
  • Rod drop monitoring: Proximity probes on piston rod packing detect rider band and piston ring wear
  • Crosshead vibration: Accelerometers on crosshead guides identify loose crosshead pins and worn bushings
  • Pressure-volume analysis: Dynamic cylinder pressure measurement provides the most comprehensive picture of compressor valve and ring condition

Pump Cavitation: Detection and Prevention

Refineries operate hundreds of centrifugal pumps, many in high-temperature hydrocarbon services where cavitation is an ever-present risk. Cavitation—the formation and violent collapse of vapour bubbles within the pump—destroys impellers, damages seals, and degrades performance.

Refinery reliability depends on catching cavitation early. Here is how:

Understanding the Causes

Cavitation occurs when the local pressure within a pump drops below the vapour pressure of the fluid being pumped. Common causes in refinery service include:

  • Insufficient NPSH available: High fluid temperatures in hot hydrocarbon services mean the fluid is already close to its boiling point, leaving minimal NPSH margin
  • Suction strainer fouling: Plugged strainers increase suction losses, reducing NPSH available
  • Operating off-design: Running pumps at flows significantly above or below their best efficiency point creates internal recirculation cavitation
  • Process upsets: Tower level changes, heat exchanger fouling, and control valve issues can alter suction conditions unpredictably

Monitoring Methods

High-frequency vibration analysis is the most effective cavitation detection method. Cavitation produces a distinctive broadband signature in the 15–100 kHz range that is readily distinguishable from mechanical defects like bearing faults or imbalance.

Ultrasonic monitoring (airborne and structure-borne) provides an even more sensitive detection method, often picking up incipient cavitation before it produces measurable vibration at standard accelerometer frequencies. Suction and discharge pressure trending, correlated with flow data, helps identify the process conditions that trigger cavitation events.

Safety-Critical Equipment: Where Monitoring Saves Lives

In oil and gas operations, equipment reliability is directly linked to personnel safety and environmental protection. Safety-critical equipment requires a monitoring philosophy that goes beyond economic optimization:

Emergency Shutdown Systems

Emergency isolation valves, blowdown valves, and depressurization systems must function on demand—but they spend most of their lives in a static state. Partial stroke testing, combined with vibration and acoustic monitoring during tests, verifies that these critical devices will perform when needed.

Flare System Integrity

Flare system compressors and knockout drums are the last line of defence for managing relief events. Monitoring these systems ensures they are always ready to handle emergency flows without becoming a hazard themselves.

Rotating Equipment in Hazardous Areas

Pumps and compressors handling flammable, toxic, or high-pressure fluids require monitoring strategies that account for the consequences of failure, not just the probability. A pump that would be classified as general-purpose in a water service may require API 670-level monitoring when handling light hydrocarbons at high temperature.

Safety principle: For safety-critical equipment, the monitoring investment decision should be based on the consequence of failure, not the cost of the equipment. A $30,000 pump handling hydrogen at 400°F deserves the same monitoring attention as a $3 million compressor if its failure could cause a loss-of-containment event.

Pipeline Monitoring for Ontario Operations

Pipeline monitoring in Ontario encompasses both transmission pipelines regulated under federal and provincial codes and facility piping within refineries and processing plants. A comprehensive approach includes:

  • Inline inspection (smart pigging): Magnetic flux leakage and ultrasonic tools detect wall loss from corrosion, cracking, and mechanical damage
  • Acoustic emission monitoring: Continuous monitoring at high-consequence locations detects active crack growth and leak initiation
  • Cathodic protection monitoring: Continuous remote monitoring of CP systems ensures corrosion protection remains effective between surveys
  • Strain monitoring: Fibre optic or conventional strain gauges at geotechnically active locations detect ground movement effects on pipeline integrity
  • SCADA integration: Real-time flow, pressure, and temperature data enables computational pipeline monitoring for leak detection

For Ontario pipeline operators, regulatory compliance under CSA Z662 and TSSA requirements drives minimum monitoring standards, but leading operators go beyond compliance to implement risk-based monitoring programs that focus resources on the highest-consequence segments.

Building Your Refinery Reliability Program

Implementing an effective condition monitoring program in a refinery requires alignment between operations, maintenance, reliability engineering, and safety teams. The key steps:

  1. Risk-based asset classification: Use API 580/581 risk-based inspection principles to rank equipment by consequence and probability of failure
  2. Technology selection: Match monitoring technology to each asset's failure modes—not every machine needs continuous online monitoring, and not every machine can be adequately covered by monthly route collection
  3. Integration with process data: Mechanical monitoring data without process context leads to false alarms and missed diagnoses. Integrate vibration, temperature, and oil analysis with DCS process data
  4. Competency development: Invest in analyst training and certification (ISO 18436 vibration analysis, STLE oil analysis). Technology without competent analysts produces data, not decisions
  5. Management of change: Ensure that process changes, operating condition modifications, and equipment alterations trigger a review of monitoring parameters and alarm setpoints

Frequently Asked Questions

What API standards govern machinery monitoring in oil and gas facilities?

API 670 is the primary standard for machinery protection systems, covering vibration, axial position, and temperature monitoring for critical rotating equipment. API 612, 617, 618, and 610 define specific requirements for turbines, compressors, and pumps respectively.

How does condition monitoring improve refinery safety?

It detects mechanical degradation before it leads to loss-of-containment events. A failing pump seal or compressor bearing detected early can be repaired in a controlled shutdown rather than failing catastrophically while processing flammable or toxic hydrocarbons.

What are the most common causes of pump cavitation in refineries?

Insufficient NPSH due to low suction pressure or high fluid temperature, plugged suction strainers, operating far from best efficiency point, and process condition changes that alter fluid vapour pressure. Cavitation is detectable through high-frequency vibration and ultrasonic analysis.

What monitoring technologies are used for pipeline integrity in Ontario?

Inline inspection (smart pigs), acoustic emission monitoring for leak detection, strain gauges at high-consequence areas, cathodic protection monitoring, and SCADA-integrated real-time flow and pressure monitoring.

How often should critical rotating equipment in a refinery be monitored?

API 670-class safety-critical equipment should have continuous online monitoring with automated protection. Important equipment should be monitored weekly to biweekly. General-purpose equipment can be monitored monthly, based on risk ranking and failure consequence severity.
