Understanding Turbine Vibration Signatures

Every turbine tells a story through its vibration. The challenge is learning to read it. Unlike slower industrial machinery, turbine-generators operate at high speeds with tight clearances, making them extraordinarily sensitive to even minor changes in condition.

The Critical Frequencies

For a 3,600 RPM turbine-generator (60 Hz synchronous speed), the key vibration components include:

  • 1× (60 Hz): The fundamental frequency. Changes here indicate mass imbalance, thermal bow, shaft bow, or coupling problems. This is your primary health indicator.
  • 2× (120 Hz): Typically indicates misalignment, cracked shaft (in severe cases), or electrical issues in the generator (stator ellipticity, unequal magnetic pull).
  • Sub-synchronous (<60 Hz): Oil whirl (~0.43×) or oil whip (~0.5× locked at a rotor natural frequency) in journal bearings. These are stability problems that can escalate rapidly.
  • Blade pass frequencies: Number of blades × RPM. Changes indicate blade damage, fouling, or erosion.

Startup and Coast-Down: The Diagnostic Gold Mine

Some of the most valuable turbine data comes not from steady-state operation but from transient events. A Bode plot (amplitude and phase vs. speed) captured during startup or coast-down reveals:

  • Critical speed locations and amplification factors
  • Changes in rotor balance state compared to previous startups
  • Bearing condition through changes in critical speed response
  • Thermal sensitivity if vibration changes significantly during heat soak

Plants that capture and archive transient data from every startup build a powerful diagnostic history. When something changes, you can overlay current data against years of baselines to pinpoint exactly what's different.

Bearing Analysis: The Foundation of Turbine Reliability

Turbine-generator bearings—whether journal (hydrodynamic), tilting pad, or rolling element—are the components most likely to cause forced outages. Their failure modes range from gradual wear to sudden catastrophic damage.

Journal Bearing Monitoring

Large turbine journal bearings operate on a thin oil film (typically 50-150 µm thick). Monitoring their health requires:

  • Shaft-relative vibration: Proximity probes (eddy current) mounted in X-Y pairs at each bearing measure actual shaft orbit. Orbit shape reveals imbalance, misalignment, rub, oil whirl, and bearing wear.
  • Bearing metal temperature: Embedded RTDs or thermocouples in the bearing babbitt detect temperature changes from increased loading, reduced oil flow, or surface damage. A sudden 5°C rise demands immediate investigation.
  • Oil analysis: Babbitt wear particles (tin, lead), viscosity degradation, water contamination, and varnish potential index all provide early warning.

Case study: A 150 MW gas turbine-generator in the Halton region showed a gradual increase in shaft orbit size at bearing #3 over six months—from 35 µm to 65 µm peak-to-peak. Oil analysis confirmed elevated tin levels. The bearing was replaced during a planned outage at a cost of $45,000. Had it failed in service, the resulting shaft damage would have required a $2.8 million rotor repair and 16 weeks offline.

Rolling Element Bearings

Where rolling element bearings are used (smaller turbines, some auxiliary equipment, generators), high-frequency enveloping analysis detects early-stage defects. The progression follows a predictable pattern:

  1. Stage 1: Subsurface fatigue—detectable only by ultrasonic or high-frequency enveloping (>20 kHz spike energy)
  2. Stage 2: Microscopic spalling—bearing defect frequencies appear in enveloped spectrum
  3. Stage 3: Visible defects—defect frequencies with harmonics and sidebands, elevated broadband noise
  4. Stage 4: Advanced damage—random broadband vibration, elevated temperature, imminent failure

The goal is always to detect problems at Stage 1 or 2, when repair can be planned weeks or months in advance.

Rotor Dynamics: Why Your Turbine Behaves the Way It Does

Understanding rotor dynamics isn't just for OEM engineers—it's essential for anyone responsible for turbine reliability. Every rotor has natural frequencies (critical speeds) that change based on bearing stiffness, seal condition, and mass distribution.

Critical Speed Management

Most large turbine-generators operate between their first and second critical speeds. The separation margin—the difference between operating speed and the nearest critical speed—must remain adequate (typically >15%). Factors that erode this margin include:

  • Bearing wear: Increases bearing clearance, reducing stiffness and lowering critical speeds
  • Seal degradation: Changes cross-coupled stiffness and damping, affecting stability
  • Coupling condition: Loose or worn couplings alter the effective shaft stiffness
  • Foundation changes: Grout degradation or structural settling changes the support stiffness

Thermal Sensitivity and Vector Tracking

Turbine rotors are long, heavy, and operate at high temperatures. Even minor asymmetries in heat distribution can cause thermal bow, which manifests as a slow change in 1× vibration vector (amplitude and phase) after load changes. Tracking the vibration vector over hours during load transitions helps distinguish thermal effects from mechanical problems like a developing crack.

A shaft crack produces a characteristic 2× component that changes with load and thermal state. If you see 2× amplitude increasing over time, especially with a phase shift, treat it as urgent—this is one of the few turbine problems that can progress from detectable to catastrophic in weeks.

Outage Planning: Where Predictive Data Pays Off

The real value of a turbine monitoring program is realized during outage planning. With comprehensive condition data, maintenance managers can make evidence-based decisions instead of following conservative OEM schedules that may not reflect actual equipment condition.

Condition-Based Outage Optimization

  • Extend intervals safely: If bearing data, vibration trends, and oil analysis all show healthy conditions, a planned outage can be deferred 6-12 months—keeping the unit generating revenue
  • Shorten scope confidently: Instead of opening every bearing "just because," focus on the bearings that data indicates need attention
  • Pre-position parts and resources: When monitoring shows a bearing trending toward alarm, you can order the replacement and schedule the crane—reducing outage duration by days
  • Coordinate with grid operators: IESO outage scheduling requires advance planning. Condition data provides the justification for your preferred outage window

Outage impact: Ontario power plants that implement comprehensive predictive maintenance typically reduce planned outage duration by 20-30% and virtually eliminate forced outages. For a 200 MW unit, each saved outage day represents approximately $200,000-400,000 in generation revenue at peak rates.

Generator-Specific Monitoring

The generator deserves its own monitoring attention separate from the turbine. Electrical and mechanical health are deeply intertwined:

  • Air gap monitoring: Capacitive sensors measuring the gap between rotor and stator detect eccentricity—both static (alignment-related) and dynamic (rotor-related)
  • Partial discharge monitoring: Detects insulation degradation in stator windings before it progresses to a ground fault
  • Shaft voltage and current: Elevated shaft voltage can cause bearing fluting—pitting of the bearing surface by electrical discharge. A simple shaft grounding brush prevents this, but monitoring confirms it's working
  • Exciter condition: Brush wear, slip ring condition, and commutator health all affect generator reliability

Building a Power Plant Monitoring Program

An effective turbine-generator monitoring program combines continuous online systems with periodic expert analysis:

  1. Online protection systems: Continuous vibration monitoring with automatic trip capability. This is safety-critical and non-negotiable.
  2. Online condition monitoring: Same sensors, but with full waveform capture, trending, and diagnostic capabilities. This is where you detect problems early.
  3. Periodic expert analysis: Monthly or quarterly review of trends by experienced analysts who understand turbine behaviour—not just vibration theory.
  4. Transient data capture: Automatic recording of startup, coast-down, and load-change events for rotor dynamics assessment.
  5. Integration with operations: Monitoring data must flow to control room operators and maintenance planners, not sit in a standalone system.
