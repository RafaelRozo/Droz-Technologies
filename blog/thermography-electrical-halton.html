Understanding Infrared Thermography for Electrical Systems

Infrared thermography uses thermal imaging cameras to detect and measure heat radiation emitted by all objects above absolute zero. Every electrical component in your facility—from the main incoming switchgear to individual motor starters—has a normal thermal signature under load. When problems develop, temperatures deviate from normal patterns in predictable ways.

What Causes Electrical Hotspots?

Thermal anomalies in electrical systems result from three primary mechanisms:

  • High-resistance connections (most common, ~70% of findings): Loose bolted connections, corroded terminals, oxidized bus bars, poor crimps, and degraded contact surfaces all increase resistance. Per Joule's law (P = I²R), heat generated is proportional to resistance times the square of the current. A connection with just 10× normal resistance carrying 200A dissipates significant excess heat.
  • Overloaded circuits (~20% of findings): Conductors, breakers, and switchgear operating above rated capacity generate excess heat across all phases uniformly. This pattern distinguishes overloading from connection problems.
  • Component deterioration (~10% of findings): Failing contactors, deteriorating insulation, damaged fuses, and internal defects produce localized heating that thermography detects before visible damage occurs.

Why Electrical Thermography Works

Infrared inspection is uniquely suited to electrical systems because:

  • Non-contact: No physical contact with energized equipment—critical for arc flash safety
  • Non-disruptive: Inspections occur while equipment is energized under normal load—no production interruption
  • Comprehensive: An experienced thermographer can inspect hundreds of electrical components per shift
  • Quantitative: Temperature measurements enable severity classification and trending over time
  • Documented: Thermal images provide visual, objective evidence for maintenance decisions and insurance compliance

NFPA 70B Compliance: What Halton Manufacturers Must Know

NFPA 70B underwent a transformative change in 2023: it was elevated from a "Recommended Practice" to a full ANSI standard. This means electrical maintenance—including thermographic inspection—is now a standard requirement rather than an optional best practice.

Key NFPA 70B Requirements for Thermography

  • Annual infrared inspections: NFPA 70B requires thermographic inspection of electrical distribution systems at least annually
  • Qualified personnel: Inspections must be performed by trained and qualified thermographers
  • Equipment under load: Inspections must be conducted with equipment energized and carrying at least 40% of rated load (preferably >60%) for meaningful results
  • Documentation: Complete records of findings, severity classifications, corrective actions, and follow-up verification
  • Condition-based maintenance: The standard emphasizes condition monitoring over time-based maintenance—thermography is a core tool

What NFPA 70B Means for Ontario Plants

For Halton Region manufacturers, NFPA 70B compliance has direct implications:

  • Insurance requirements: Ontario commercial insurers are rapidly incorporating NFPA 70B into their loss prevention requirements. Plants without documented annual thermographic inspections face higher premiums or coverage restrictions.
  • ESA compliance: While the Ontario Electrical Safety Authority (ESA) enforces the Ontario Electrical Safety Code (OESC), NFPA 70B compliance demonstrates due diligence for electrical maintenance—a factor in any regulatory inquiry after an incident.
  • Liability protection: Documented thermographic inspections demonstrate that facility management took reasonable steps to identify and correct electrical hazards—essential defense in liability claims.

Insurance Requirements for Electrical Thermography in Ontario

Major commercial and industrial insurers operating in Ontario increasingly mandate infrared thermographic inspections:

FM Global (Factory Mutual)

FM Global Data Sheet 5-20 (Electrical Testing) explicitly requires infrared thermographic surveys of electrical distribution systems. The standard specifies:

  • Annual thermographic surveys of all switchgear, panelboards, and distribution equipment
  • Surveys must be performed by qualified Level I or Level II thermographers
  • Reports must include thermal images, temperature measurements, severity classifications, and corrective action recommendations
  • Follow-up inspections to verify repairs

Other Major Insurers

Zurich, AIG, Chubb, Hartford Steam Boiler, and other carriers serving Ontario industrial accounts all have thermography provisions in their loss prevention programs. Common requirements include:

  • Annual infrared surveys as a condition of full coverage
  • Remediation of all Priority 1 and Priority 2 findings within specified timeframes
  • Documentation retained for audit purposes
  • Penalties or premium surcharges for non-compliance

Understanding the ΔT Method

The ΔT (delta-T) method compares the temperature of a suspected anomaly against a reference—either an identical component under similar load (comparative method) or ambient temperature. The comparative method is more accurate because it accounts for normal heating under load.

For example, if Phase A of a three-phase breaker connection reads 85°C while Phases B and C read 55°C, the ΔT is 30°C—a Priority 2 (serious) finding requiring prompt repair.

Common Electrical Findings by Component

  • Bolted bus connections: Loose bolts at bus joints and tap connections—the single most common finding. Even 1/4 turn of loosening can cause significant heating.
  • Circuit breaker connections: Line and load connections on breakers frequently develop high resistance due to thermal cycling and vibration.
  • Fused disconnects: Fuse clips, fuse contacts, and blade connections are common hotspot locations.
  • Motor control centers (MCCs): Stab connections, overload relays, and starter contacts require regular inspection.
  • Transformers: Connection points, tap changers, and cooling system anomalies.
  • Cable terminations: Lug connections, compression fittings, and cable entries at junction boxes.

The Thermographic Inspection Process

Pre-Inspection Planning

Before arriving at your Halton facility, Droz Technologies coordinates with your electrical team to ensure:

  • Equipment list: Inventory of all electrical assets to be inspected (switchgear, panelboards, MCCs, transformers, disconnects)
  • Single-line diagram review: Understanding of the electrical distribution architecture
  • Load conditions: Scheduling the inspection during periods of representative load (minimum 40% rated, ideally 60%+)
  • Access planning: Ensuring all panels and enclosures can be safely opened for inspection
  • Safety protocols: Arc flash hazard analysis review, required PPE levels, and facility safety orientation

Arc Flash Safety Considerations

Infrared inspection of energized electrical equipment requires strict adherence to arc flash safety per CSA Z462 (Workplace Electrical Safety) and IEEE 1584. Droz Technologies thermographers:

  • Review arc flash labels on all equipment before opening enclosures
  • Wear appropriate PPE based on incident energy levels (Cal/cm²)
  • Maintain safe approach distances per CSA Z462 table boundaries
  • Use IR windows where installed to eliminate the need to open enclosures
  • Coordinate with facility electricians for panel opening and closing

Inspection Execution

Using professional radiometric thermal cameras (minimum 320×240 resolution, ±2°C accuracy), our thermographers systematically inspect each component:

  1. Visual inspection: Check for visible signs of overheating (discoloration, melted insulation, arc tracking marks)
  2. Thermal scanning: Image all connections, conductors, and components under load
  3. Anomaly identification: Flag any component with ΔT above threshold
  4. Detailed imaging: Capture both thermal and visual (digital) photographs of every finding
  5. Temperature measurement: Record peak temperature, reference temperature, ΔT, and emissivity used
  6. Classification: Assign priority level per NETA MTS criteria

Reporting

Droz Technologies delivers a comprehensive thermographic inspection report within 5 business days, including:

  • Executive summary: Total components inspected, number of anomalies by priority level, overall system condition assessment
  • Detailed findings: Each anomaly documented with thermal image, visual image, location, component identification, temperature data, severity classification, and recommended corrective action
  • Trending data: Comparison with previous survey results (for repeat clients) showing improvement or deterioration
  • Equipment inventory: Complete list of inspected assets for maintenance records
  • Compliance documentation: Report formatted to meet NFPA 70B and insurance carrier requirements

Infrared Windows: Enhancing Safety and Efficiency

Infrared windows (IR viewports) are permanently installed crystal or polymer ports in electrical enclosures that allow thermographic inspection without opening panel covers. Benefits include:

  • Eliminates arc flash exposure: No need to remove covers from energized equipment
  • Faster inspections: Reduce inspection time by 50–75%
  • More frequent monitoring: Enables monthly or weekly spot checks by plant personnel
  • Consistent imaging: Same viewing angle every time enables accurate trending

Droz Technologies recommends IR windows for all critical switchgear (main incoming, tie breakers, critical process feeders) and high arc-flash-hazard enclosures. The investment typically pays for itself within 2–3 survey cycles through reduced inspection time and improved safety.

Integrating Thermography with Your Maintenance Program

Electrical thermography delivers maximum value when integrated into a comprehensive condition monitoring program:

  • Vibration analysis: For motor-driven equipment, combine electrical thermography with vibration monitoring to cover both electrical and mechanical failure modes
  • Ultrasound: Airborne ultrasound detects electrical discharge (arcing, tracking, corona) that thermography may miss in early stages—the two technologies are complementary
  • Oil analysis: For oil-filled transformers, combine thermographic inspection with dissolved gas analysis (DGA) for comprehensive transformer health assessment
  • Power quality analysis: Harmonic distortion and power factor issues cause additional heating—power quality measurements help explain unexpected thermal anomalies

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is infrared thermography essential for electrical systems?

Approximately 30% of industrial fires originate in electrical systems, and most are preceded by thermal anomalies detectable weeks or months in advance. Thermography provides non-contact, non-disruptive inspection of energized equipment during normal production.

What is NFPA 70B and how does it relate to thermography?

NFPA 70B was elevated to a mandatory standard in 2023, requiring annual infrared thermographic inspections of electrical distribution systems. Many Ontario insurers now require NFPA 70B compliance as a condition of coverage.

How are thermographic hotspots classified?

Using the ΔT method per NETA MTS standards: Priority 4 (1–10°C above reference) — monitor. Priority 3 (11–20°C) — repair within 30 days. Priority 2 (21–40°C) — repair ASAP. Priority 1 (>40°C) — immediate action, imminent failure risk.

Does insurance require infrared thermography in Ontario?

Many commercial insurers including FM Global, Zurich, and others require or strongly recommend annual thermographic inspections. Non-compliance can result in higher premiums, reduced coverage, or claim denials after an electrical fire.

How often should electrical thermography be performed?

NFPA 70B requires annual inspections minimum. For critical systems and 24/7 operations, semi-annual or quarterly inspections are recommended. Droz Technologies can design a frequency schedule appropriate for your facility's risk profile.

What qualifications should a thermographer have?

Level I certification minimum (per ASNT SNT-TC-1A or ISO 18436-7), with Level II preferred for electrical inspections. They must understand electrical systems, heat transfer, and arc flash safety per CSA Z462.
